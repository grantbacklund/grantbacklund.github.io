Hierarchical and empirical Bayesian estimation of NBA three point percentages

Dec 17, 2021 3 min read

Consider the problem of estimating an NBA player’s three point field goal percentage $\theta_i$ for the current season based on his percentage $Y_i$ from last season, where $i = 1, \ldots, N$ and $N$ is the total number of players. The maximum likelihood estimate (MLE) of $\theta_i$ is just $Y_i$. We explain how to improve upon the MLE in terms of both estimation accuracy and uncertainty quantification by considering two types of Bayesian estimators which utilize information from other players to impose shrinkage towards a global mean.

Joint estimation of all players' three point percentages boils down to the classical problem in statistics of estimation of the multivariate normal mean. More formally, the setup is as follows.

  1. Conditional on $\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_N$, let $Y_1, \ldots, Y_N$ be independent with $Y_i \sim \hbox{N}(\theta_i, \sigma^2)$, $i = 1, \ldots, N$, $\sigma^2 > 0$ being known. Without loss of generality, assume $\sigma^2 = 1$.
  2. The $\theta_i$s have independent $\hbox{N}(\mu_i, A)$ priors, $i = 1, \ldots, N$. Write $\theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_N)^T, \mu = (\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_N), Y = (Y_1, \ldots, Y_N)^T$ and $y = (y_1, \ldots, y_N)^T$.

The posterior distribution of $\theta$ given $Y = y$ is then $\hbox{N}((1-B)y + B \mu, (1-B) I_N)$, where $B = (A + 1)^{-1}$. Accordingly, the posterior mean (the usual Bayes estimate) of $\theta$ is given by \begin{align} E(\theta | Y = y) = (1-B)y + B \mu. \label{eq1} \end{align}

In an empirical Bayes (EB) or a hierarchical Bayes (HB) scenario, some or all of the prior parameters are unknown. In an EB setup, these parameters are estimated from the marginal distribution of $Y$ which in this case is $\hbox{N}(\mu, B^{-1} I_N)$. An HB procedure, on the other hand, models the uncertainty of the unknown prior parameters by assigning distributions to them. Such distributions are often called hyperpriors.

For us, the most salient case is when $\mu_1 = \ldots = \mu_N = \mu$ (say), where both $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$ and $A > 0$ are unknown. Marginally, $Y \sim \hbox{N}(\mu 1_N, B^{-1} I_N)$, with $B = (A + 1)^{-1}$. Hence, $\left( \bar{Y}, \sum_{i=1}^N(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2 \right)$ is complete sufficient, so that the UMVUEs of $\mu$ and $B$ are given respectively by $\bar{Y}$ and $(p - 3) / \sum_{i=1}^N(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2$. Substituting these estimators into (\ref{eq1}), the EB estimator of $\theta$ is given by

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{EB}}$$

This is a modification of the James-Stein estimator that was introduced by Lindley (1962). Whereas the original James-Stein estimator shrinks Y towards a specified point, the modified estimator in (\ref{eq2}) shrinks Y towards a hyperplane spanned by $1_N$. The estimator $\hat{\theta}_{\text{EB}}$ is known to dominate $Y$ for $N \ge 4$.
